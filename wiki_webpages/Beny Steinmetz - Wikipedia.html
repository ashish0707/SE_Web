Beny Steinmetz (Hebrew: בני שטיינמץ‎‎; born 1956) is an Israeli businessman, with a portfolio in diamond-mining, engineering and real estate.[1][2][3]

Early life[edit]

Beny Steinmetz was born in Netanya, Israel in 1956, the fourth child of Rubin Steinmetz.[4] He inherited the Geneva-based Steinmetz Diamond Group from his father.[1]

Beny Steinmetz Group Resources[edit]

Steinmetz along with his brother Daniel founded what subsequently became known as Beny Steinmetz Group Resources (BSGR). Beny currently occupies an advisory role to the company's Board of Directors.[3] In March 2014, the Swiss daily Le Temps reported that Beny Steinmetz had sold shares in the Steinmetz Diamond Group (SDG) to his brother Daniel.[5][6]

BSGR in Guinea controversy[edit]

In December 2008, a three-year exploration permit for Simandou Blocks 1 & 2, in The Republic of Guinea, was awarded to BSGR Guinea, after the government of Guinea, under its then president, General Lansana Conté, ordered that it be relinquished by its previous holder, the British-Australian multinational mining company Rio Tinto Group.[7][8][9] Conté signed over the rights to mine the northern half of Simandou, days before he died, to Steinmetz for $160 million. Steinmetz then soon sold a 51% share on to Vale for $2.5 billion.[10]

The U.S. Justice Department and the FBI have been investigating BSGR's acquisition of the rights to extract half of the iron ore deposits at Simandou, Guinea, due to concerns about corruption and bribery.[9][11][12][13] BSGR have stringently denied these allegations, and in an interview to the New Yorker Beny Steinmetz has said: “We are the victims. We have done only good things for Guinea, and what we’re getting is spit in the face."[14]

In April 2014 the Guinean government announced that it had "precise and coherent evidence" that BSGR had obtained the Simandou mining rights by paying bribes to the wife of then president Lansana Conté in 2008, and that the rights would be stripped from BSGR and its partner Vale S.A..[15] In September 2014 BSGR started international legal proceedings in arbitration against the Republic of Guinea, to challenge the decision to revoke its mining rights.[16]

Rio Tinto then filed suit against Steinmetz, BSGR and Vale alleging that they had devised a RICO scheme to steal "Rio Tinto's valuable mining rights".[17] Steinmetz subsequently hired former FBI director Louis Freeh, defense attorney Alan Dershowitz, law firm Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom as part of his defense team.[18]

In November 2015 BSGR won the dismissal of the Rio Tinto RICO lawsuit.[19] U.S. District Judge Richard Berman ruled that Rio exceeded the statute of limitations when filing their claim against BSGR in 2014 and that the company failed to identify a pattern of racketeering activity by the defendants.[20]

In mid-November 2016 Alan Davies, head of the Rio Tinto department responsible for Simandou, was suspended due to an investigation into the lawfulness of Rio Tinto's payment to Francois de Combert[21] – former managing partner at Lazard and personal adviser to the president of Guinea.[22] On December 1, 2016 France 24 aired a pair of recordings from 2012 that it claimed were conversations between Francois de Combret and unnamed people involved in the two competing negotiations over the future of Simandou.[23] Australian media has referred to the controversy as 'Guineagate'. [24]

Other business ventures[edit]

He is also an investor of Gabriel Resources Ltd.[25]

Koidu Holdings, which formerly operated as a joint venture, has been wholly owned by the BSGR company since 2007.[26][27] In 1997, he founded STI Ventures NV, a venture capital firm that invests in start-up companies in Israel.[28] In 1999, he was the owner of Tucows.[29] BSGR and another investment firm, IMR, also control Cunico. A now-defunct company, of which he was a founder and shareholder, Nikanor Plc, listed in London, was acquired by Katanga Mining in 2008.[30] With his company called Scorpio, he owns real estate in Kazakhstan, Russia, and Eastern Europe.[1][2]

On March 10, 2016, he was indicted in absentia by Romanian National Anticorruption Directorate, on charges regarding the unlawful recovery of Paul-Philippe Hohenzollern's inheritance.[31] The court in Romania dismissed the request for a warrant for his arrest.[32]

Net worth[edit]

The March 2011 issue of Forbes magazine estimated his personal wealth at US$6 billion.[33] As of March 2011, he was the second richest person in Israel, before Sammy Ofer died in June 2011.[34] According to Forbes in June 2014, Steinmetz is the sixth richest Israeli in the world with a net worth of $3.5 billion.[35]

Personal[edit]

Steinmetz lives in Geneva, Switzerland.[9] He is married with four children.[1] With his wife, he oversees the Beny & Agnes Steinmetz Foundation, which donates to schools, hospitals, army units and the arts in Israel.[1] He also holds French citizenship and describes himself as an "international Israeli."[36]
